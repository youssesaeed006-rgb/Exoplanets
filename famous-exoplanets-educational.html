Kepler-452b
✦ Habitability Score 7/10

Kepler-452b

Earth's Cousin
  • ✦ Distance: 1,400 light-years
  • ◈ Star Type: G-type (similar to our Sun)
  • ⬡ Year Length: 385 Earth days
  • ✦ Size (Radius): ~1.6x Earth's radius (Super-Earth)
  • ◈ Discovery: 2015 by NASA Kepler Space Telescope
  • ⬡ Zone Quality: Lies in the habitable zone of its star — liquid water could exist

Kepler-452b is one of the most Earth-like planets ever found. It orbits a star very similar to our Sun at nearly the same distance. Scientists believe it could have a rocky surface, active volcanoes, and possibly even an atmosphere. It is the closest match to Earth in size and orbit ever confirmed.

TRAPPIST-1e
✦ Habitability Score 9/10

TRAPPIST-1e

The Habitable Hope
  • ✦ Distance: 39 light-years
  • ◈ Star Type: Ultra-cool red dwarf
  • ⬡ Year Length: ~9.2 Earth days
  • ✦ Size (Radius): ~0.92x Earth's radius (slightly smaller than Earth)
  • ◈ Discovery: 2017 by the TRAPPIST telescope (Belgium)
  • ⬡ Zone Quality: Strong candidate for liquid water and Earth-like conditions

TRAPPIST-1e is one of seven rocky planets orbiting the tiny red star TRAPPIST-1, and it sits in the most promising part of its habitable zone. Being relatively close to Earth at just 39 light-years, it is a top target for the James Webb Space Telescope to study its atmosphere. Scientists consider it one of the best candidates for extraterrestrial life ever identified.

55 Cancri e
✦ Habitability Score 1/10

55 Cancri e

The Diamond World
  • ✦ Distance: 41 light-years
  • ◈ Star Type: G-type binary star
  • ⬡ Year Length: ~18 Earth hours
  • ✦ Size (Radius): ~2x Earth's radius (Super-Earth)
  • ◈ Discovery: 2004 by Confirmed 2011
  • ⬡ Zone Quality: Not habitable — surface temperature ~2,400°C (4,350°F)

55 Cancri e is a super-Earth so close to its host star that a year lasts less than a day. Its surface is believed to be covered in molten lava oceans. Fascinatingly, the planet's interior may be rich in carbon under extreme pressure — creating conditions where diamonds could form deep beneath the surface. The James Webb Space Telescope detected hints of a volatile atmosphere in 2024.

K2-18b
✦ Habitability Score 8/10

K2-18b

The Ocean World
  • ✦ Distance: 124 light-years
  • ◈ Star Type: Red dwarf (M-type)
  • ⬡ Year Length: ~33 Earth days
  • ✦ Size (Radius): ~2.7x Earth's radius (Sub-Neptune)
  • ◈ Discovery: 2015 by Kepler K2 mission
  • ⬡ Zone Quality: Possible "Hycean" world — a planet with a hydrogen atmosphere over a vast liquid water ocean

K2-18b made global headlines in 2023 when the James Webb Space Telescope detected carbon dioxide, methane, and possibly dimethyl sulfide in its atmosphere — a molecule on Earth produced only by living organisms. While not confirmed, this makes K2-18b one of the most talked-about planets in the search for life beyond Earth.

LHS 1140b
✦ Habitability Score 9/10

LHS 1140b

The Quiet Guardian
  • ✦ Distance: 49 light-years
  • ◈ Star Type: Quiet red dwarf (M-type)
  • ⬡ Year Length: ~25 Earth days
  • ✦ Size (Radius): ~1.7x Earth's radius (Super-Earth)
  • ◈ Discovery: 2017 by MEarth Project
  • ⬡ Zone Quality: Top-ranked candidate for a rocky habitable world with a stable atmosphere

LHS 1140b orbits a remarkably calm red dwarf star — making it special because most red dwarfs blast their planets with intense radiation. Its quiet star, combined with its position in the habitable zone and hints of a nitrogen-rich atmosphere detected by JWST, make it arguably the most promising rocky world for habitability known today.

Understanding Cosmic Habitability Indices

Earth is our baseline for comparison, but the universe is filled with incredible diversity. Telescopes like Kepler, Trappist, and now the powerhouse James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) utilize light transit spectroscopy to analyze compounds in the atmospheric layers of planets trillions of miles away.

Our habitability scores represent a comparative metric factoring in stellar emission stability, atmospheric thermal equilibrium, and surface rock configuration. From scorching lava oceans to possible diamonds and vast waterworlds, these five planets exemplify the magnificent exoplanetary frontier.

Defining the Cosmic Distance: What is a Light-Year?

Click to Reveal Discovery Details

A light-year is the speed at which light travels in one Julian year—nearly 6 trillion miles (9.46 trillion km). Traveling at the speed of Voyager 1 (the fastest spacecraft leaving our system), it would take about 70,000 years to reach the nearest exoplanet, Proxima Centauri b!

Cosmic Census: How Many Planets Have Been Found?

Over 5,500 exoplanets are officially confirmed in NASA's Database, which is only a minute fraction of the billions of planets suspected to belong to the Milky Way. Every star in our night sky is believed to host at least one exoplanet!

Goldilocks Zone: Circumstellar Habitable Ranges

Click to Reveal Discovery Details

The habitable zone is the orbital band around a star where thermodynamic equilibrium allows surface pressures capable of sustaining liquid water. It isn't too hot, and isn't too cold—satisfying the conditions needed for structural carbon biology.

Detection Methods: Finding Invisible Worlds

Click to Reveal Discovery Details

Exoplanets are usually too dim to see directly. Most are discovered via the Transit Method (observing small periodic dips of a star's brightness) or the Radial Velocity Method (observing gravitational wobbles in a star's spectral signatures).

The Wanderers: Rogue Planets Drift in Solitude

Click to Reveal Discovery Details

Rogue planets are worlds expelled during early solar dynamics that wander through interstellar space in absolute isolation, orbiting no parent star. It is believed rogue planets may actually outnumber stars in our galaxy!
